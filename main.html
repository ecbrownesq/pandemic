What you need to know about COdA-403

Updated by Julia Belluz on April 10, 2018, 11:30 a.m. ET @juliaoftoronto

Tweet Share
Tweet Share on Twitter Share Share on Facebook +
LinkedIn Share on LinkedIn Email Email Print Print

A few days ago, someone purporting to work for the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, going by the handle Velociraptor05, leaked a trove of internal documents. These documents paint a troubling picture of an agency in over it's head, having trouble properly dealing with the various new diseases hitting the world this year.

At this point, treatment appears to have largely stopped working

The worst of it involves the virus known as COdA-403 (often shortened to COdA). Thus far, it's been confined to the Middle East, but the CDC appears concerned that it now has the potential to spread globally. In addition, they're now searching out a person of interest known only as K. Miles. Miles purportedly knows more about COdA than he or she should know. An anonymous source at INTERPOL has confirmed that they are indeed looking for Miles, and while they are not ruling out foul intent, Miles is not yet considered a suspect.

But for now, the new revelations about COdA are troubling. Here's what you need to know.

What is COdA?

COdA-403 is the name of a series of viruses that emerged in a Middle East epidemic back in January. The virus has evolved at an alarmingly rapid pace since discovery, leading to a number of classifications of the virus. The current strain is classified as COdA-403c, marking the second major evolution in less than four months. At the rate it's going, we'll have to update this very soon with further classifications. But as of April 10, 2018, here's a breakdown of each strain of the virus:

  • COdA-403a was first confirmed in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia on January 17. At this stage, victims suffer intense flu-like symptoms, and require careful treatment (think something similar to the Ebola outbreak in 2014).
  • COdA-403b is the first evolution of the virus, first discovered in Algiers on February 2. At this point, treatment appears to have largely stopped working. Victims of this strain remain quarantined in hospitals two months after first contracting it. Researchers are working hard on a cure, but they're not optimistic.
  • COdA-403c is the latest evolution. This evolution was first discovered in a CDC laboratory, and we probably would not have heard about it if not for the leaked memo. We now know of a small number of people in Delhi, India suffering from this strain, and with this one, the symptoms worsen to a rather ghastly degree.

Some researchers speculate that the A strain was not the initial one, evolving from something else. However, that would be difficult to confirm because you'd be looking at anybody suffering from flu-like symptoms back in the middle of flu season. In any case, that first strain makes for a good starting point because it marks the initial point where researchers recognized that something serious was unfolding.

What makes COdA so deadly?

Reading about the current symptoms is enough to make one want to retch. After a short incubation period, the skin of victims turns translucent, showing the viscera and tissue beneath. If that weren't enough, victims display erratically aggressive behavior, flying into a hallucinogenic rage at a seemingly random pattern. They do have periods in which they act (but don't look) like normal humans. Due primarily to the skin condition, a number of people have referred to victims as "Faded." Editorially speaking, I can't condone this terminology, but we have caught a few people at the CDC and WHO referring to victims as such. The term is probably here to stay, and despite my reservations, I'm going to use it for now.

Anything you could imagine in response to the term "nightmare scenario" is probably a reasonable guess

As bad as the symptoms are, what's more concerning is the rate at which the virus is evolving, and how much it's changed in each evolution. We're still two evolutions behind on our ability to properly treat the virus, and given the pessimism on the B strain, we may fall even further behind as the virus continues to evolve. Given how horrible the symptoms are now, it's difficult to properly imagine things two months from now. Anything you could imagine in response to the term "nightmare scenario" is probably a reasonable guess.

Why is the outbreak in Delhi important?

On April 14, one of the Faded went on a rampage through Connaught Place in Delhi. Police ultimately shot and killed him, but not before he killed four and injured another 17. Eight of those injured have gone on to become Faded themselves. All of them remain in quarantine.

This was the first time the public was exposed to what victims of COdA have become. Those suffering from the B strain have been kept under strict quarantine by the WHO, and some critics speculated that the WHO had something to hide. The Delhi attack seems to confirm these fears.

Nobody in the press has been able to get the WHO to comment since the attack. CNN received a statement from the CDC stating that the Faded are indeed suffering from the C strain of the virus, and that nothing of the sort is happening to victims of the B strain. The CDC has not commented further.

Why are officials so cagey about discussing COdA?

The most obvious answer is that they do not have a good handle on dealing with it.

This may sound like a sensationalist damning of how the CDC and WHO have handled this epidemic, but the leaked documents specifically state that the CDC is, at best, in a state of triage. As such, they have probably made a calculated decision to avoid sharing any information that isn't good news unless it's exceptionally vital in order to avoid panic. If so, then this strategy backfired when the leaks made the panic that much worse.

Another reason might be simple politics. President Trump has shown in the past few months that he simply doesn't care about COdA. For the time being, COdA appears to be confined to Asia, which is far enough away from the United States. Presumably he thinks that anyone infected won't take an international flight across continents.

What important things do we not know?

In short: a lot. Perhaps most urgently, we don't know the method of transmission. Quarantines have been largely ad hoc in nature thus far, and have proven to be reasonably successful. But if, say, the virus is airborne, then we need to be doing much more in order to prevent the spread of it.

We don't know how long symptoms are expected to last. In the case of most viruses, the human immune system starts to develop defenses against the virus in the form of antibodies. This effectively neuters the effects of the virus, allowing the victim to start recovering. In the case of victims of the B and C strains, we have not seen any improvement in victims. This feeds into fears of a worst-case scenario, which is something like HIV. That virus can undergo what is known as an "escape mutation" which allows it to evade the antibodies the immune system creates. If COdA can do that, then victims may well be stuck with it for life.

Ominously, we don't know what the journal page found in Delhi means. The CDC leak suggested that whoever wrote it did not work for them and knew things about it that were not otherwise public knowledge. The page seemingly links it to Bootyscoops, another virus that has hit the world hard this year. The name K. Miles also appears on it. We don't know if this person wrote the journal, is an independent researcher, bioengineered the virus, or anything else.

Just how screwed are we?

I don't want to start sounding apocalyptic, but this is easily the biggest public health challenge we've been up against in at least a century. Without an imminent breakthrough in finding a cure, this virus is very scary.

Some medical professionals are using the Black Death disease as a point of comparison for COdA. For reference, that disease killed approximately half of the European population over the course of four years in the fourteenth century. Obviously it's too soon to tell if this comparison is fair, but the fact that people in the who are making this comparison should go to show just how serious this virus is.
